Social Media, Compensation & Power of Group Think

Since I don't use social media apps. I have to reach out to friends and family to get the specific details of trends of the "culture". The trends of the "ideal" woman should be thin, with a thigh gap. A young man should have a chiseled jaw line, less than 10% body fat, and find a way to afford grass fed meats. The pressure to be more without having to tools and emotional intelligence to sustain that lifestyle seems to be overwhelming to many who choose that route to fit in.

For the young women who decide to try out for the DCC, they are put through a grueling schedule of training, choreography, makeovers, one on one sessions with directors, being scheduled for trips to churches and media training. They also hold down 2 or three jobs to make ends meet because they are only paid $15 a game ( this has now been changed to $75 a game). Although they have negotiated a pay increase they do not have health insurance and they are still an independent contractor in the eyes of the Dallas Cowboys.

The trends like #ThinTok, explicitly promote extreme thinness and restrictive eating, sometimes masquerading as "wellness" or "healthy lifestyle" advice. These trends can create dangerous echo chambers where disordered behaviors are normalized or even glorified. While some platforms attempt to moderate such content, the sheer volume and the rapid evolution of hashtags make complete control challenging. This environment can be particularly harmful to young people, whose identities and self-perceptions are still developing and who are highly susceptible to peer and media influences.(Read more about the #SkinnyTok trend on Parents.com)

The pressure is not limited to "influencers"; even seemingly positive trends, like those promoting extreme fitness or "clean eating," can inadvertently lead to **Orthorexia**, an obsessive preoccupation with healthy eating that becomes detrimental. The constant need for validation through likes and comments further fuels a cycle of comparison and anxiety about appearance. - Mother Jones Article

Conclusion

Understanding the profound influence of cultural norms and social media on body image and eating disorders is the first step towards prevention. Strategies must focus on fostering body positivity and neutrality, promoting intuitive eating, and encouraging joyful movement. Building media literacy skills is essential to help individuals critically evaluate the content they consume and resist harmful ideals. Embracing the diversity of human bodies, celebrating health at every size, and valuing well-being over aesthetics are crucial for cultivating a healthier relationship with food and self in a media-saturated world.
