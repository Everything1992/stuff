My Personal Strategy for Preventing Disordered Eating:

At this moment in time, I have a positive view of my body, and my strategy is to maintain this perspective by continuing to eat intuitively, engage in joyful movement, and harness the principles learned from my upbringing and academic studies. While my BMI is considered normal, my doctor's instruction to ease up on wine (due to sugar levels not aligning with other results) was a subtle reminder that even health-focused choices require mindfulness to avoid slipping into disordered patterns.

  1. Prioritizing Mindful Consumption and Intuitive Health Choices:

    My doctor's suggestion to reduce wine intake, while a change, has become an opportunity to reinforce intuitive eating principles. By focusing on balanced meals with vegetables, proteins, and enjoying mocktails, I'm actively avoiding the restrictive mindset that often leads to deprivation and rebound eating. This directly counters the negative impacts of **chronic dieting** and the dangers of **calorie-restrictive diets**, which our course has highlighted as major causes of binge eating disorder. I'm learning to listen to my body's needs and pleasurable alternatives, rather than adhering to rigid external rules or labeling foods as "forbidden."

  2. Embracing Joyful Movement and Rejecting Performance Extremes:

    Another core strategy is to continue engaging in physical activities like beach volleyball, surfing, and paddleboarding. My motivation stems from the genuine joy, energy, and mental clarity these sports provide for my overall well-being and to stay focused in my cybersecurity work. This approach actively resists the tendency for some athletes, which we discussed in class, to push workouts to extremes, often leading to destructive patterns of **binge-eating, compulsive workouts, and fasting**. This is not a path I intend to follow; instead, I prioritize feeling grounded and vibrant through movement, rather than using it as a tool for punishment or extreme aesthetic goals.

  3. Fostering Cultural Resilience and Critical Media Literacy:

    My conversations with my friend Matilde, discussing how French women are often conditioned to stay thin due to fashion industry pressures, have been insightful. This contrasts sharply with the body ideals prevalent in my African American/Brazilian community, where a "soda bottle" shape is celebrated. This rich cultural perspective, reinforced by planning my trip to Bahia for Carnival 2026 (an event full of joyous dancing, eating, and community without body image worries or Instagram pressures), is a powerful preventative measure. It helps me critically evaluate societal messaging, such as the "SlimKim" effect and "ThinTok", which promote unrealistic beauty standards and can lead to **internalized weight bias** and **body dissatisfaction**. This cultural grounding helps me avoid falling into patterns like **Orthorexia**, a focus on "pure" eating to extreme, which I subconsciously recognized and avoided during my time in Southern California, where such fixations seemed prevalent. I refuse to obsess over food contents, valuing instead its role in nourishment, pleasure, and connection.
