Eating Disorders: How Culture & Social Media Create Them

The image above is of Victoria Kalina, a former Dallas Cowboys Cheerleader. She has been openly vocal about her struggles with body image and mental health during and after her tenure. Her journey, including details shared in the Real Pod interview, highlights the intense pressures on athletes and performers regarding body standards and the psychological impact of public scrutiny in demanding fields.

"Petite & Proportionate": The Ideal Body in Culture

The Netflix documentary detailing the Dallas Cowboys Cheerleaders' process of choosing and eliminating prospective candidates for the squad encapsulates many of the issues addressed in this class. In an episode in Season 1, Kelli Finglass (Director of the Dallas Cowboys Cheerleaders) repeatedly emphasized and visibly conveyed her desire for a "petite & proportionate" look for the Dallas Cowboys Cheerleader costumes. Kelli and others on the administrative panel made clear that this "ideal aesthetic" should be the foundation of "privilege" to wear that DCC uniform.

As I was playing this series in the background while doing this final assignment, I was also revisiting my notes from Lindo Bacon, PhD's book. The cultures are different, but the results for the communities within them are the same: a group of people feels the need to offer themselves up to a culture that tells them they are not good enough until they "submit" in totality is crippling to the self-esteem.

In the 2010s, the Dallas Cowboys Cheerleaders would be weighed often. The uniforms are fitted to the body, so if their weight fluctuates, they would be ridiculed and questioned about their eating habits. Bacon has been vocal about his own struggles about body image. Bacon is thorough about the constant state of 'doubt': Do I look okay? Do I sound okay? Will they like me? Do they accept me in this room? Oddly, Ava from Season 2 Episode 1 said the same thing – not knowing what to do when trying to navigate a system that does not value you, but only the image of you.

Social Media: The Amplifier and the Trap

Social media platforms have become powerful amplifiers of these cultural body ideals, acting as both a mirror and a magnifier for comparison and self-criticism. Apps like TikTok, Instagram, and Facebook expose users to a relentless stream of curated, often filtered or surgically enhanced, images and videos that present highly stylized and often unattainable body types as the norm. This constant visual bombardment can intensify body dissatisfaction, a known precursor to disordered eating.

Specific trends, such as **#SkinnyTok** or **#ThinTok**, explicitly promote extreme thinness and restrictive eating, sometimes masquerading as "wellness" or "healthy lifestyle" advice. These trends can create dangerous echo chambers where disordered behaviors are normalized or even glorified. While some platforms attempt to moderate such content, the sheer volume and the rapid evolution of hashtags make complete control challenging. This environment can be particularly harmful to young people, whose identities and self-perceptions are still developing and who are highly susceptible to peer and media influences.

The pressure is not limited to "influencers"; even seemingly positive trends, like those promoting extreme fitness or "clean eating," can inadvertently lead to **Orthorexia**, an obsessive preoccupation with healthy eating that becomes detrimental. The constant need for validation through likes and comments further fuels a cycle of comparison and anxiety about appearance.

Impacts and Pathways to Disordered Eating

  • **Internalized Weight Bias:** Continuous exposure to thin ideals and weight-related stigma can lead individuals to internalize negative beliefs about their own bodies, fostering self-criticism and shame.
  • **Chronic Dieting & Restriction:** The pursuit of the "ideal" body often leads to chronic dieting and severe food restriction. As studies show, this can trigger a dangerous cycle of deprivation, intense cravings, and eventual bingeing, significantly increasing the risk for eating disorders like Bulimia Nervosa and Binge Eating Disorder.
  • **Teasing & Fat-Shaming:** Cultural messaging about body ideals can translate into direct negative experiences, such as teasing or fat-shaming, which are well-documented as direct causes of body dissatisfaction and eating disorder development.
  • **Mental Health Decline:** The constant pressure and comparison fueled by culture and social media contribute to increased anxiety, depression, low self-esteem, and social isolation, all of which are comorbid conditions or direct pathways to eating disorders.
  • **Unrealistic Expectations in Activities:** Even in professional or high-performance fields like cheerleading or high-level athletics, the emphasis on a specific "look" can lead to unhealthy practices, as seen in patterns of bingeing, compulsive workouts, and fasting among some athletes.

Moving Forward: Fostering Body Positivity & Resilience

Understanding the profound influence of cultural norms and social media on body image and eating disorders is the first step towards prevention. Strategies must focus on fostering body positivity and neutrality, promoting intuitive eating, and encouraging joyful movement. Building media literacy skills is essential to help individuals critically evaluate the content they consume and resist harmful ideals. Embracing the diversity of human bodies, celebrating health at every size, and valuing well-being over aesthetics are crucial for cultivating a healthier relationship with food and self in a media-saturated world.
